Functions: Understanding Rates of Change

 

Average Rate of Change

The average rate of change at which one value within a function changes in relationship to another. The average rate of change is usually used to determine the slope of a graphed function.

 

The average rate of change equation. (Image provided from https://tutorme.com/blog/post/average-rate-of-change/.)

 

Since linear rate of changes are always straight lines, their average rate of change will remain constant on the set of points chosen into the slope formula.

 

Images provided by https://www.desmos.com/calculator and https://tutorme.com/blog/post/average-rate-of-change/.

 

A nonlinear function does not have a constant rate of change however. It will have a different slope depending on the points used in the average rate of change formula.

 

In this case, form a straight secant line segment that goes through two points, and you can use the average rate of change formula.

Images provided by https://www.desmos.com/calculator and https://tutorme.com/blog/post/average-rate-of-change/.

 

Instantaneous Rate of Change

The instantaneous rate of change measures the rate of change, or slope, of a curve at a certain instant. It is the same as the change in the derivative value at a specific point.

 

Trigonometric Functions

 

The trigonometric functions are real functions which relate an angle of a right-angled triangle to ratios of two side lengths.

Unit-Circle Definitions

The six trigonometric functions can be defined as coordinate values of points on the Euclidean plane (a flat two-dimensional surface that extends indefinitely) that are related to the unit circle, a circle of radius one centered at the origin ) of this coordinate system. While the right-angled definitions above allow for the definition of trigonometric functions for angles between 0 and  radian(90°), the unit circle definitions allow the domain of trigonometric functions to be extended to all positive and negative real numbers.

Trigonometric Identities and Equations

 

Trigonometric identities are equalities that involve trigonometric functions and are true for every value of the occurring variables for which both sides of the equality are defined. These identities are useful whenever expressions involving trigonometric functions need to be simplified.
