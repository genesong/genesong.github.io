Discrete Functions: Sequences and Series & Financial Applications

 

Sequences and Series

 

A sequence is an ordered list of numbers, and a series is a sum of a sequence of terms (a term is a number in a sequence.) In other words, a series is a list of numbers with addition operations between them.

 

Arithmetic Sequence

A sequence that has the same difference, the common difference, between any pairs of consecutive terms (Nelson Textbook).

Image provided from https://www.onlinemathlearning.com/arithmetic-sequences-nth-term.html

 

Recursive Sequence

A sequence for which one term (or more) is given and each successive terms is determined from the previous term(s) (Nelson Textbook).

 

 

Image provided from https://calcworkshop.com/combinatorics/recursive-formula/

 

General Term

A formula, labelled tₙ,, that expresses each term of a sequence as a function of its position. For example, if the general term is tₙ = 2n, then to calculate the 12th term (t₁₂), substitute n = 12. t₁₂ = 2(12) - 24 (Nelson Textbook).

 

Recursive Formula

A formula relating the general term of a sequence to the previous term(s) (Nelson Textbook).

 

 

Image provided from https://www.khanacademy.org/math/algebra/x2f8bb11595b61c86:sequences/x2f8bb11595b61c86:constructing-arithmetic-sequences/a/writing-recursive-formulas-for-arithmetic-sequences

 

Geometric Sequence

A sequence that has the same ratio, the common ratio, between any consecutive terms (Nelson Textbook).

 

Image provided from https://www.onlinemathlearning.com/geometric-sequences-nth-term.html

Discrete Functions: Financial Applications

 

Definitions provided by the Nelson Textbook:

●     Principal: a sum of money that is borrowed or invested.

●     Interest: the money earned from an investment or the cost of borrowing money.

●     Amount: the total value of an investment or loan. The amount is given by A = P + 1, where A is the amount, P is the principal, and I is the interest.

 

Simple Interest

Interest earned or paid only on the original sum of money that was invested or borrowed.

 

 

Image provided from https://www.businessinsider.com/simple-interest

 

Compound Interest

Interest that is added to the principal before new interest earned is calculated. So interest is calculated on the principal and on interest already earned. Interest is paid at regular time intervals called the compounding period (Nelson Textbook).

 

Future Value

The total amount, A, of an investment after a certain length of time (Nelson Textbook).

 

Present Value

The principal that would have to be invested now to get a specific future value in a certain amount of time. PV is used for present value instead of P, since P is used for principal (Nelson Textbook).

 

i = interest rate per compounding period

n = number of compounding periods

 

Image provided from https://www.educba.com/time-value-of-money-formula/

 

Annuity

A series of payments or investments made at regular intervals. A simple annuity is an annuity in which the payments coincide with the compounding period, or conversion period. An ordinary annuity is an annuity in which the payments are made at the end of each interval. Unless otherwise stated, each annuity in this chapter is a simple, ordinary annuity (Nelson Textbook).

 

 

 

i = interest rate per compounding period
